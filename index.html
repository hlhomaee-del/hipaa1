HIPAA — The Big Picture
The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) sets national standards for protecting patient health information. It has two major rules relevant to billers: the Privacy Rule (who can access and share patient info) and the Security Rule (how electronic health info must be protected). Covered entities and their business associates must comply.
PHI — What It Is
PHI (Protected Health Information) is any information that can identify a patient AND relates to their health condition, healthcare received, or payment for care. PHI can be written, spoken, or electronic. Key identifiers include: name, date of birth, SSN, address, account number, dates of service, and diagnosis codes when linked to an individual. A de-identified record with all identifiers removed is NOT PHI.

Permitted Use vs Disclosure
HIPAA allows PHI to be used or disclosed without patient authorization for Treatment, Payment, and Healthcare Operations (TPO). For example, sending a claim to an insurance company is permitted without extra patient consent because it is for payment purposes. Sharing PHI for non-TPO purposes (such as marketing or releasing records to an employer) generally requires a signed patient authorization.

Consent — Two Types
Informed Consent is a patient's voluntary, documented agreement to undergo a medical procedure after being fully informed of the risks, benefits, and alternatives. It must be written. Implied Consent occurs when a patient's actions suggest agreement — such as extending an arm for a blood draw without objection, or presenting for treatment in an emergency when they cannot speak. Implied consent is generally not enough for surgical procedures.
Authorization — HIPAA-Specific
A HIPAA Authorization is a formal, signed permission document allowing a covered entity to use or disclose PHI for purposes beyond TPO. It must include: the specific information to be disclosed, who is receiving it, the purpose, an expiration date, and the patient's right to revoke. An authorization is required when sending records to an attorney, life insurer, or employer.

Staff must verify identity and check form completeness before releasing any PHI.

Fraud — Intentional Deception
Fraud is an intentional act. It involves knowingly and willfully making false statements or misrepresentations to obtain payment. Key word: knowingly. Examples: billing for services never performed, falsifying diagnosis codes to justify a procedure, or billing for a higher-level service than was actually provided — when done deliberately. Fraud can result in criminal prosecution, civil penalties, and exclusion from federal programs.
Abuse — Improper Pattern Without Proven Intent
Abuse involves billing practices that are improper or wasteful but do not necessarily involve intentional deception. Examples: consistently billing for services not medically necessary, charging above the allowable fee schedule, or routinely waiving copayments without documented hardship. Abuse can be corrected through education and process improvement. It may cross into fraud if intent is later proven.

Upcoding — Billing Higher Than Performed
Upcoding means assigning a higher-level code than the service actually documented and performed — to receive a greater reimbursement. Example: A patient has a brief 10-minute, low-complexity visit. The biller submits a code for a comprehensive, high-complexity visit. The code does not reflect the actual service. This is a form of fraud when done intentionally and is a common target of Medicare audits.
Unbundling — Splitting What Should Be One Code
Unbundling occurs when services that should be billed under a single comprehensive code are instead billed as multiple individual component codes — inflating the reimbursement. Example: A surgical package includes pre-op, surgery, and post-op. Billing each component separately instead of the global surgical code is unbundling. The CCI (Correct Coding Initiative) edits exist specifically to detect unbundling patterns.

The False Claims Act — What It Is
The False Claims Act (FCA) is a federal law that makes it illegal to knowingly submit false or fraudulent claims to the federal government for payment — including Medicare and Medicaid. It applies to any person or organization that submits, causes to be submitted, or conspires to submit a false claim. Violations can result in civil penalties per false claim plus treble damages (three times the amount the government was defrauded).
Qui Tam — Whistleblower Provision
The FCA includes a qui tam provision that allows private individuals — including employees — to file a lawsuit on behalf of the government if they discover fraud. The whistleblower (called a "relator") may receive a portion of any funds recovered. This encourages insiders to report fraud. Retaliation against whistleblowers is prohibited under the FCA.

Compliance Programs — Preventing Violations
A healthcare compliance program helps organizations prevent FCA violations and HIPAA breaches. The OIG (Office of Inspector General) identifies seven core elements of an effective compliance program: written policies and procedures, a compliance officer/contact, staff education and training, effective lines of communication, internal monitoring and auditing, enforcement of standards, and prompt response to detected problems.

⚠️ Test Trap: FCA Scope
The False Claims Act applies to claims submitted to federal programs (Medicare, Medicaid, TRICARE, etc.) — not directly to private insurance. However, in practice, fraudulent billing behaviors often affect all payers. CBCS questions will typically anchor FCA scenarios to Medicare or Medicaid — if you see "private insurance only," that's a distractor. The FCA also does NOT require proof that the patient was harmed — only that a false claim was knowingly submitted.

🪤 TRAP PAIR: Consent vs Authorization

🪤 TRAP PAIR: Use vs Disclosure of PHI
Why students miss this: Both involve PHI being accessed or shared — but in different directions.

Use: PHI is accessed or shared WITHIN the covered entity (internal).
Disclosure: PHI is shared OUTSIDE the covered entity (external — to another provider, payer, attorney, etc.).

The clue: Ask yourself — did the information leave the organization? If yes → disclosure. If it stayed inside → use.

🪤 TRAP PAIR: HIPAA Violation vs Workflow Error
Why students miss this: Not every mistake in a medical office is a HIPAA violation. HIPAA violations involve PHI being improperly accessed, used, or disclosed. A billing entry error, scheduling mistake, or filing error that does NOT expose patient information is a workflow problem — not a HIPAA issue.

The clue: Ask — was PHI improperly seen, shared, or accessed by an unauthorized party? If yes → HIPAA concern. If no → workflow/administrative issue.

HIPAA & PHI — Rapid Reference
HIPAA PRIVACY RULE
Governs who can access, use, and disclose PHI. Requires minimum necessary standard. Provides patients rights to access their records.
HIPAA SECURITY RULE
Governs electronic PHI (ePHI). Requires administrative, physical, and technical safeguards to protect ePHI.
PHI — WHAT IT IS
Individually identifiable health information related to health status, care, or payment. Includes 18 types of identifiers (name, DOB, SSN, address, account #, dates, etc.).
TPO EXCEPTIONS
Treatment · Payment · Healthcare Operations. PHI may be used/disclosed for these purposes WITHOUT patient authorization.
USE vs DISCLOSURE
Use = PHI accessed/shared WITHIN the covered entity.
Disclosure = PHI shared OUTSIDE the covered entity.
MINIMUM NECESSARY
Only the minimum PHI needed to accomplish the task should be used or disclosed. Never send more than required.

Fraud · Abuse · Upcoding · Unbundling
🔴 FRAUD
Knowingly and intentionally submitting false claims or misrepresentations to receive improper payment.

Clue word: "knowingly," "intentionally," "deliberately"
🟡 ABUSE
Improper billing practices resulting in unnecessary costs without proven intent to defraud.

Clue words: "pattern," "consistently," "without documentation"
📈 UPCODING
Billing a higher-level code than the service actually performed or documented.

Clue: One code, wrong (higher) level
📦 UNBUNDLING
Billing component codes separately when one global/comprehensive code should cover all.

Clue: Multiple codes instead of one global code
⚖️
False Claims Act — Quick Reference
WHAT IT COVERS
False or fraudulent claims submitted to federal programs — Medicare, Medicaid, TRICARE.
QUI TAM
Employees can report fraud on behalf of the government and may receive a share of recovered funds. Whistleblowers are protected from retaliation.
PENALTIES
Civil penalties per false claim PLUS treble damages (3× the amount defrauded). Can result in exclusion from federal programs.
DOCUMENTATION RULE
"If it isn't documented, it didn't happen." Always submit claims supported by complete clinical documentation.

Top CBCS Test Traps
Trap 1 — Consent ≠ Authorization
A general consent form does NOT authorize non-TPO disclosures (employer, legal, research). Those require a separate, signed HIPAA authorization.
Trap 2 — Use ≠ Disclosure
Use = internal (within the entity). Disclosure = external (outside the entity). If it leaves the organization → disclosure.
Trap 3 — Fraud ≠ Abuse
The clue is intent. "Knowingly" = fraud. Pattern without proven intent = abuse. Same billing problem, different legal classification.
Trap 4 — Upcoding ≠ Unbundling
Upcoding = one wrong (higher) code. Unbundling = multiple codes for what should be one global code.
Trap 5 — FCA only covers federal programs
Private insurance fraud is still fraud — but the False Claims Act specifically targets Medicare/Medicaid/federal program claims.
Trap 6 — System access ≠ authorized access
Having login access to a system does not mean every record is accessible. A job-related need is required under the Minimum Necessary standard.
Trap 7 — Urgency is a distractor
When a scenario mentions that something is urgent or the requester is in a rush — that is almost always a distractor designed to make you skip proper authorization steps.
